pH

pH is a measure of the acidity or alkalinity of water and is measured on a scale of 1 to 14.

If the water is neither acid nor alkaline it is neutral and is expressed as a pH of 7.

The water that we drink out of the tap is usually neutral.

If the water in a stream is too acid or too alkaline it can kill all aquatic life.

HARDNESS


Water described as hard is high in naturally occurring dissolved minerals, specifically calcium
and magnesium carbonates (chalk).

Hard water can lead to the formation of soap scum (preventing soaps from foaming), and may leave
white deposits on hard surfaces or cause scaling problems inside hot water systems and kettles.

WH is classified into soft water (60 mg/L or less), moderately hard water (60–120 mg/L), hard water
(120–180 mg/L), and very hard water (over 181 mg/L) by the World Health Organization (WHO) [1,4].

SOLIDS

Solids in water quality metrics measure the concentration of suspended or dissolved particles,
indicating water quality. Elevated levels may suggest contamination, impacting clarity, aquatic life,
and potentially posing risks to human health.

CHLORAMINES

Chloramines are chemical compounds formed by the combination of chlorine and ammonia in water
treatment. They are commonly used as disinfectants in drinking water to control the growth of
harmful microorganisms. Chloramines offer a more stable and longer-lasting disinfection effect
compared to free chlorine but can affect water taste and may lead to potential health concerns
if present in high concentrations.

An optimal level, typically recommended to be below 4 mg/L by health authorities, ensures
proper disinfection without compromising taste or posing health risks. Local regulations
should be consulted for specific guidelines.

SULPHATE

Sulfate is a chemical compound present in water and, while it is generally considered safe,
the optimal level is context-dependent, with guidelines often suggesting concentrations below
250–500 milligrams per liter to prevent potential laxative effects.

ORGANIC CARBON

Organic carbon in water refers to the presence of carbon-containing compounds derived from living
organisms, and the optimal level depends on water treatment goals, with lower concentrations desired
for drinking water to minimize the risk of disinfection by-products.

TRIHALOMETHANES

Trihalomethanes are chemical by-products formed when chlorine reacts with organic matter in water;
the optimal level for these disinfection by-products is typically regulated to be below 80 micrograms
per liter in drinking water to minimize potential health risks.

TURBIDITY

Turbidity in water refers to the cloudiness caused by suspended particles, and the optimal level is
generally set below 1 nephelometric turbidity unit (NTU) for drinking water to ensure clarity and comply
with water quality standards.

POTABILITY

Potability in water indicates its suitability for human consumption, and the optimal level is met when
the water meets or exceeds drinking water quality standards, ensuring it is safe, free from contaminants,
and meets health regulations.
